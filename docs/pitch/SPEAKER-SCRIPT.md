# Woahh — Co-Founder Pitch: Speaker Script

---

## SLIDE 1 — Cover

> "Thanks for making the time. What I'm going to show you today is a product that already exists, unit economics that already work, and a mission I genuinely believe no other SaaS company has had the courage to build.

> Woahh gives small businesses — the café on your corner, the family restaurant, the independent boutique — the complete digital infrastructure that billion-dollar chains have. For less than $10 a day.

> I'm not here to pitch you on an idea. I'm here because I want to find the right person to build this with me. Let me show you what that looks like."

---

## SLIDE 2 — The Problem

> "Every major restaurant chain in the world runs a full technology stack. Dominos has its own app. McDonald's owns its customer data, runs loyalty programs, sends push notifications, re-engages lapsed customers with targeted offers.

> Small businesses have a Square terminal and a Facebook page.

> And the gap isn't staying the same — it's compounding. Every year, big brands build deeper customer relationships. Small businesses don't own a single email address. They don't know who visited twice and stopped coming. They can't send a birthday reward.

> Meanwhile, the platforms they depend on are extracting value at every step."

---

## SLIDE 3 — The Cost of Status Quo

> "Let's put numbers on it. A small restaurant doing $50,000 a month in sales — that sounds healthy — is currently paying somewhere between five and six thousand dollars a month just to exist digitally.

> That's Square fees, Uber Eats commissions at 25 to 30 percent, a website that can't take orders, and a custom app they almost certainly can't afford.

> They're paying more than most of them earn in net profit — just to be found and to take orders. And they still don't own their customer data at the end of it."

---

## SLIDE 4 — The Solution

> "Woahh gives a small business owner the same tech stack that Dominos spent millions building — for a flat monthly fee starting at $49.

> Founding merchants pay zero commission — and after that, a low 3% on marketplace orders, a fraction of the 25–30% the delivery apps take. No per-device hardware costs. No developer required.

> Their own branded storefront. A full loyalty and rewards engine. CRM with complete customer history. SMS and email marketing. Kitchen display. Table management. Delivery orchestration via Uber Direct at a flat fee, not a percentage.

> Same technology. A fraction of the cost. Available to anyone."

---

## SLIDE 5 — Plans & Pricing

> "We have four tiers. Solo at $49 for the smallest operators. Marketplace at $89 — our most popular — which unlocks CRM, loyalty, SMS, and the discovery marketplace listing. Growth at $150 adds up to seven locations, priority placement, custom domain and PWA, and higher messaging caps. And Enterprise for multi-location chains.

> Every plan includes a fixed monthly charitable donation built in — I'll come back to that.

> The headline that sells itself: 60-day free trial, full Marketplace access, no credit card required. We're betting on the product."

---

## SLIDE 6 — Unit Economics

> "The model works at every scale. After the charity split, contribution margins run north of 90 percent pre-payroll — the marginal cost to serve a merchant is only a few dollars.

> Our infrastructure is multi-tenant SaaS. We build once and serve many. Adding merchant number 500 costs almost the same as adding merchant number 50. Supabase, Resend, Clicksend — the marginal cost per merchant is around $2.80.

> Take a Growth-tier merchant doing $15,000 a month. Their $150 subscription splits 50/50, so we keep $75 — plus about 2% of their GMV in net commission, roughly $300. That's about $375 a month to Woahh per merchant, against a cost to serve of a few dollars.

> We are profitable from merchant number one."

---

## SLIDE 7 — The Giving Model

> "Here's the part most SaaS companies would bury in a footnote. We give half of everything to charity — half of every subscription and half of every order fee. Not as a campaign. Not as a marketing line. As a structural commitment.

> Half of every subscription goes to charity — that's $24.50 on Solo, $75 on Growth. And on every online order the merchant pays 3% and the customer pays 1%; half of that — 2% of the order — goes to charity, and Woahh keeps the other half. Every dollar is routed to a charitable cause, fully publicly verifiable on the impact ledger.

> A merchant processing $15,000 a month through Woahh sends roughly $345 to charity automatically — about $300 from commission plus $45 from their subscription. Their storefront displays an Impact Partner badge. Their customers can see it.

> At 5,000 merchants averaging $15,000 in monthly GMV, that's over $20 million a year to charitable causes.

> No competitor is within an order of magnitude of this commitment."

---

## SLIDE 8 — What's Built

> "I want to be clear about where we are. We are not here asking for support to start. The product exists.

> Order management, kitchen display, storefront, CRM, loyalty engine, SMS and email campaigns, table management, reservations, delivery integration, multi-tenant isolation, demo mode — all live and working. I can open it on this screen right now.

> What remains: the /eat marketplace UI — the database and API foundation are complete, the UI is pending. Stripe billing. A public impact portal. Advanced analytics.

> And one more thing.

> [Pause.]

> The app is fully functional right now. The single remaining non-technical decision is the name. The moment we decide — together — the domain goes live and email infrastructure follows the same day. That's not a gap. That's a first decision for us to make as partners."

---

## SLIDE 9 — Growth Model

> "The numbers compound quickly.

> At 100 merchants, we're generating around $14,900 a month in recurring revenue — profitable from day one.

> At 1,000 merchants, that's $149,000 a month in MRR and $298,000 a year going to charity.

> At 5,000 merchants — conservative blended averages across tiers — we're at $745,000 a month in recurring revenue and $1.49 million a year in charitable impact.

> The infrastructure scales on its own. The work is merchant acquisition and the people who make that happen."

---

## SLIDE 10 — Why We Win

> "Let me address the competitive landscape directly.

> Square and Toast give you operational tools. No marketplace discovery.
> Uber Eats and DoorDash give you discovery. They take 30% and you never own the customer.
> Nobody does both axes under one subscription.

> But the moat isn't just the product. It's data lock-in. A merchant who has built their customer list in Woahh's CRM, run loyalty campaigns, integrated their delivery flow — they don't leave easily. The switching cost is their customer relationship itself.

> And the giving model is a growth engine, not a cost centre. A café owner who tells three peers 'this platform donates to charity and costs less than my phone bill' is worth more than any Google ad."

---

## SLIDE 11 — Go To Market

> "We're starting in Brisbane. That's a deliberate choice, not a limitation.

> Brisbane has a dense, independent hospitality and retail scene that is genuinely underserved by enterprise software. It's close enough to manage relationships directly at launch. It's large enough to generate real proof points — GMV data, case studies, press coverage — without the noise and cost of Sydney or Melbourne.

> A Woahh with 200 Brisbane merchants, $4 million in monthly GMV, and a visible charitable impact story sells itself into the next market.

> Phase two is East Coast expansion to 1,000+ merchants. Phase three is the national /eat marketplace launch and global localisation."

---

## SLIDE 12 — Who We're Looking For

> "The technology scales. The infrastructure is built. What this needs now is the right person alongside it.

> Merchant acquisition — relationships in hospitality or retail, or the ability to build a sales motion. The product sells itself; we need someone who starts the first conversations.

> Operator mindset — turning a working product into a repeatable process. The gap between 10 merchants and 200 is systems, not code.

> Market expansion — localisation, compliance, partnerships. If you've scaled into new markets before, you know what breaks first.

> Brand and consumer audience — building the /eat side. The merchant story is compelling. The consumer brand needs to be built.

> I'm not looking for someone to execute a plan. I'm looking for someone who looks at this and sees what they'd change, what they'd add, and where they'd take it."

---

## SLIDE 13 — Close

> "We have the product. We have the model. We have the mission.

> [Pause.]

> What do you think? What would you change? What do you bring to this?"
